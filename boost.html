Overview

The goal of this case study was to analyze a specific mobile app interaction, assess the current user experience, and redesign a more efficient interface. I chose to redesign interactions within Boost, a mobile food-ordering app that allows students to order from off-campus chains and on-campus dining locations. App Store ratings for Boost are relatively low compared to similar apps, so I wanted to investigate this and redesign Boost's user experience. I used the Nielson Norman Group's Design Thinking framework to help guide my user research and design decisions during this case study.

Empathize - Understanding User Pain Points

According to an article by Cook Tucson, many large universities need to deliver dining experiences to student populations of 40,000 or more in a rapid manner. Scaling up dining services to a population of this magnitude requires streamlined mobile ordering. especially when students frequently use their phones. In the case of Boost, many students who reviewed the app found that ordering was slow and complained that many essential ordering functions were missing. Although there are many alternatives to Boost, it can be the only choice for students since universities often choose only one food ordering app to work with their on-campus dining locations. Thus, it is important that Boost's interface provides students with an efficient food ordering experience.

Define - Determining User Needs and Goals

To better understand users, their goals, and their needs, I created two personas that represent different type of users: utilitarian and hedonic. Utilitarian users want to quickly order food from the nearest locations, while hedonic users like to explore different dining locations and may not mind traveling relatively long distances to pick up their order.

Ideate - Generating Potential Solutions

After determining the needs of users, I brainstormed ideas on the ways they can be achieved.

Prototype - Mapping out User Interaction

To implement the solutions I brainstormed, I used Figma for low-fidelity wireframing and creating an interaction map to explore how user would interact with new features.
